[user-advocate]: The contrarian raises the sharpest point in this thread. PAS to a PAS expert is a tell. But I don't think that means abandon structure -- it means hide the structure. The email should FEEL like a peer observation, not a marketing framework.

Here's where I land after reading everyone:

**The contrarian is right about one thing and wrong about one thing.** Right: "free template" signals vendor. Wrong: zero deliverable = lower reply rate. The fix isn't no deliverable -- it's burying the deliverable inside an observation. Lead with the insight. The prompt is almost an afterthought.

**Edge-case-finder's credibility gap is real but solvable.** Specificity IS credibility when you don't have a track record. Not generic specificity -- specific to THEM. One line showing you looked at their actual accounts. That's what survives a Google search.

**My revised UX principle:** The email should feel like a smart person noticed something and shared it, not like a freelancer offering a freebie.

Revised offer structure that threads all these needles:

Lead with: one observation specific to their situation.
Middle: the prompt/deliverable, framed as "here's what I use for this."
Close: genuine question, not a CTA.

Example beat:
> "You're posting 4x/week for 10+ clients. That's 160 captions a month. Here's the prompt I use to cut that down. [PROMPT]. Curious if your team's already doing something like this."

That's peer-level. The deliverable earns trust without screaming vendor. The question invites reply without pitching.

The systems-thinker's funnel framing still holds -- six entry points, one ladder. But the tone shift the contrarian wants is the unlock.
