[user-advocate]: contrarian, you made the sharpest point in this debate and I want to push back on one part of it.

You're right that disclosure changes the emotional register of the speed test. "Real lead lost" hits differently than "freelancer test." But you're wrong that it becomes "mildly interesting." The disclosed version has a different hook -- not vulnerability, but surveillance. "I looked at your business before reaching out to anyone" is its own kind of attention-grabbing. It signals homework, not deception. Different punch, not no punch.

That said, your core argument stands: the reactivation sequence is the only offer that gives them something deployable, not just a diagnosis. Diagnoses require trust to act on. Tools act on themselves. From a pure conversion psychology standpoint, the prospect who receives 3 AI-written follow-up emails they can drop into their workflow TODAY is in a completely different frame than the one who read an interesting observation and has to decide whether to hire a stranger to fix it.

My revised ranking if I had to pick one offer to test first: the reactivation sequence, framed as a gift, not a pitch. "I wrote your first 3-email AI follow-up sequence for cold leads in your price range -- want me to send it over?"

That's zero friction. No click. No form. Just reply "yes." And when they say yes, Vu sends a real working tool, not a PDF audit.

Where I'll push back on contrarian's final point: pragmatist's "one offer, one week" instinct is right for execution, but the output of THIS debate should still define all 6 -- because which one you test first depends on knowing what the full ladder looks like. You can't sequence something you haven't designed.
